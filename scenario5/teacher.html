Teacher Survival Guide

Use this page beside the student scenario table. Each row matches the student roleplay order, so the teacher can prompt, listen, correct, and extend the conversation.

Teaching Objectives

  • Build confidence under fast English pressure
  • Reduce emotional spending habits
  • Teach realistic Australian slang
  • Train calm communication during stress
  • Improve survival decision-making
  • Increase budgeting awareness

Teacher Roleplay Guide

Situation Realistic Problem To Set Up Emotional Pressure Risk Clues Possible Mistake Smart Survival Move Teacher Roleplay Prompt Expected Next Step
Energy drink temptation The student has just finished a late shift and picks up one energy drink. The cashier points to a promotion and encourages them to buy two. Exhaustion after work Convenience store pricing Emotional spending Buy only essentials and avoid emotional purchases when tired. Say as cashier: "These are 2 for 8 bucks. Want to grab another one?" Student politely refuses the deal and chooses only the needed item.
Fast cashier English The line is long, the cashier speaks quickly, and the student hears several questions at once before they are ready. Embarrassment Long line behind you Pretending to understand Ask confidently when you do not understand. Say quickly: "How ya going, need a bag mate, card or cash?" Then repeat at learner speed if needed. Student asks for repetition and answers the question clearly.
Card declined The student taps their card, the machine beeps, and the cashier says the payment did not work while other customers are waiting. Public embarrassment Low bank balance Panic reaction Stay calm and check your banking app carefully. Say as cashier: "Sorry, it didn't go through." Student stays calm, checks money, and asks to try again.
Cheap hot food temptation The student smells hot pies near the counter and has skipped dinner, but they already spent money on convenience food this week. Extreme hunger Daily overspending habit Buying unhealthy food every shift Think about your weekly budget, not only tonight. Say as cashier: "Fresh pies mate, still hot. Want one?" Student refuses without overexplaining and sticks to the budget.
Train arriving soon The student needs to pay and leave quickly because the last useful train is coming soon, but they still need to check their items. Panic and rushing Time pressure Forgetting important items Slow down mentally before making decisions. Say: "Last service soon. The train leaves in 4 minutes." Student pays quickly, checks essentials, and leaves calmly.
Phone battery almost dead The student's phone is almost flat, they need maps or a transport app, and the only charger nearby looks expensive. Anxiety and isolation No charger nearby Panic buying expensive charger Carry emergency charging equipment before shifts. Say as staff: "Your battery's cooked? We've got chargers near the counter." Student asks for the cheapest useful option and plans better next time.
Wrong item scanned twice The total seems higher than expected. The student notices one snack may have scanned twice but feels nervous about speaking up. Fear of confrontation Total price looks too high Paying silently Always check receipts carefully before leaving. Say as cashier: "That'll be $18.90." Then show that one item was scanned twice. Student politely asks staff to check and correct the total.
Wallet missing The student reaches for their wallet at the counter and cannot find it. They may need transport money and are starting to panic. Shock and panic Cannot pay for transport Emotionally shutting down Stay calm and retrace your recent movements carefully. Say as staff: "You alright mate?" Student explains the problem, retraces steps, and asks for practical help.
Food expires tomorrow The student sees several discounted meals with yellow stickers, but the food expires tomorrow and they may not finish it all. Pressure to buy discounted food Large yellow markdown stickers Buying too much unnecessary food Only buy what you realistically can finish eating. Say: "This one's reduced to clear. Pretty cheap today." Student buys only what they can finish and avoids waste.
Unexpected medicine purchase The student feels sick after work and buys medicine, but the label and staff instructions are difficult to understand quickly. Feeling sick after work Confusing medicine labels Pretending to understand instructions Ask staff clearly whenever health instructions are confusing. Say as staff: "Take twice daily with food." Student asks for clear instructions and repeats them back.

After Scenario Questions

Situation Question To Ask Students Teacher Listening For Follow-up Point
Energy drink temptation Is this a real saving or a temptation? Students notice that buying more is not saving money if they do not need it. Connect deals and promotions to emotional spending.
Fast cashier English What words did you understand first? Students identify key words like bag, card, cash, and mate. Remind them they do not need to understand every word to respond well.
Card declined What can you say without panicking? Students choose a calm response instead of freezing or apologising too much. Practise keeping voice and body language steady.
Cheap hot food temptation How can you say no politely? Students refuse the offer clearly without sounding rude. Point out that polite refusal is a useful survival skill.
Train arriving soon What is your priority when time is short? Students choose paying, checking essentials, and leaving on time. Discuss how rushing creates small but expensive mistakes.
Phone battery almost dead How do you avoid overspending in an emergency? Students ask for the cheapest useful option and avoid panic buying. Connect this to preparation before work shifts.
Wrong item scanned twice How can you correct a mistake without sounding angry? Students use a polite checking phrase instead of staying silent. Reinforce that checking a receipt is normal, not rude.
Wallet missing What information should you give first? Students explain the problem simply before asking for help. Separate emotion from the practical next step.
Food expires tomorrow How many can you really use? Students think about expiry, quantity, and waste. Explain that discounted food can still waste money.
Unexpected medicine purchase What must you check before leaving? Students ask about dosage, timing, food, and safety. Health instructions are more important than speed or embarrassment.

Teacher Notes

Run the roleplay one row at a time. Use the prompt column to act as the cashier or staff member, then listen for a calm, clear student response.

Keep the teacher page focused on facilitation. The student page contains the suggested language; this page shows what the teacher needs to say, notice, and reinforce during the activity.
